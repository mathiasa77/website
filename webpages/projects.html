NASA Micro-G NExT Challenge 1

Lead Mechanical Engineer

August 2020 - June 2021

Design and Simulation Manufacturing Final Testing Detailed PDFs

In the 2021 NASA Micro-G NExT Challenge, Prompt 1, collegiate-level teams from across the country competed to design an autonomous drone capable of triangulating and traversing to a distress beacon in an ocean environment. This mission was aimed to aide in search and rescue efforts for the NASA Artemis Program by delivering crew survival aide equiptment.

Myself and the team of 24 UC Berkeley students had our proof of concept design accepted and approved at the end of 2020, eliminating some 20 other university teams, and leaving us to compete against the remaining 8. Beginning in early 2021 we started construction of our design: the Berkeley Autonomous Emergency Responder, or BEAR. Once construction and testing was finished, the BEAR was sent to NASA's Nuetral Bouyancy Lab, NBL, in Houston, Texas to complete testing among NBL engineers.

Below you'll find a litte more information regarding the BEAR and my involvement as the Lead Mechanical Engineer.

Design and Simulation

The BEAR was designed to fulfill specific mission parameters such as: calculate a heading given a mission specific radio frequency, traverse turbulant water carring a payload of rescue-specific materials (such as water, a first-aid kit, an inflatable life-preserver, etc), and visually identify and deliver the payload to the recuee(s).

After spending weeks deliberating over prelimiary design ideas ranging from a fully submerged hull to a pontoon-style hull, a self-righting monohull design was selected upon engineering design considerations (laid out further in the design matrix linked in the SOMETHING document). With a length, width, and height of approx. 32in x 15.5in x 9.5in, the main structure was to be made from a carbon-fiber and epoxy-resin composite.

The BEAR was modelled completely in Solidworks, an industry-grade CAD software. In utilizing CAD we were able to accurately model weight distribution, bouyancy, payload organizaion, and electrical component integration.

One of the central design parameters laid in the challenge description was that the BEAR had to structuraly withstand a 10-15 foot drop into the water. From this, we used ANSYS finite-element analysis (FEA) software to simulate impact performance on the lower-portion of the hull shell. Gathering stress and strain tolerance values was vital in understanding structural rigidity before construction began just a few months later.

The BEAR had to also support its own weight from three O-ring hooks at the top of the hull structure. The expected pressure resulting from the holes of the top hull structure and the flanges matting the top and bottom hull shells together were also simulated in ANSYS FEA software to construct an accurate prediction of stress and strain on the body. These simulations further validated material selection and interior structural bracing.

Furthermore, it was vital gather data regarding the dynamic performance in the water of the BEAR before contruction. This was done by using NUMECA's computational fluid dynamics (CFD) software to simulate the hydro-dynamic pressure on the hull at a variety of velocities and accelerations. The models gathered from this gave the electrical and programming team a range of values to work within when programming the motor thrust values.

Manufacturing: Carbon-Fiber Composites

In order to meet design requirements the team had to build a drone that was structurally tough (the ability to absorb energy and plastically deform without fracture/failure) and rigid yet lightweight enough to fall within the design parameters. Given this, a carbon fiber composite material was selected.

Four layers of 3k 2x2 twill 5.9oz carbon fiber cloth with a marine-grade epoxy resin matrix yeilded an acceptable balance between monitary afforability, ease of manufacturing, materials attainability, and mechanical effectiveness. The team was not only satisfied with the benefits of this material, but was rather excited to learn about how to manufacture composite parts and how to impliment the vacuum-bag process: a valuable skill I hope to apply in my professional career.

Manufacuring the hull structure using the vacuum-bag process meant that we also had to manufacture two molds for the composite to imprint upon. Stacked medium-density fiber wood sheets served as the blocks of mold material for the CNC router to carve out of. Autodesk Fusion 360 computer-aided manufacturing (CAM) software was used to program the CNC router path.

As was the Lead for the Mechanical Engineering team, some of my central duties were researching and leading the vacuum-bag processes/procedures, material allocation, and coordinating manufacturing sessions.

Breifly, we had to first prepare the mold surface with PVA mold release liquid so the final composite could be seperated, apply successive of layers of pre-calculated ratios of carbon fiber cloth and epoxy resin, apply the low-temperature release film and breather/bleeder cloths, apply the vacuum bag film, and finally asssemble the vacuum plumbing. Sustaining a constant 11psi using a 2.5 CFM vacuum pump we were able accomplish proper infusion of the expoxy resin with the carbon fiber cloth, resulting in a a rigid structure.

Over the span of a few weeks the mechanical team was able to lay-up a water-tight and structurally-sound carbon fiber hull. The following two weeks would be spent manufacturing and fixing the additional hardware to the hull.

All in all, it was a pleasure learning how to utilize this practice and I couldn't be more proud of the commitment the team put in to accomplish the mission.

Manufacturing: Electronics Integration

The mechanical team also had the responsability of manufacturing the electronics housing and integrating electrical components. Modelling in CAD allowed the team to utilize additive manufacturing technology to 3D print lighweight components such as the electonics housing structure, lid mounting brackets and shims.

I personally found that the electronics box was a rather fun assembly to engineer. Nearly all of the electrical components and computers, or 'brains' as the electrical lead would say, would be placed on a sled with a gasketted door.

The modularity this design offered would streamline the programming and assembly process and would relieve the team from wasting valuable time troubleshooting electrical hardware during testing.

Final Iteration Testing

Testing and validation took place in the few weeks leading up to the day we would mail the BEAR to NASA in Housin. Between meeting the challenge of trying to organize COVID-safe locations for the team to test in appropriately sized bodies of water, and schedule times for teammembers to meet during the very busy weeks leading up to finals, we would inevitably have a decently successful testing campaign.

To say it was satisfying to finally see our creation perform adequetly would be an understatement, rather, it was invigerating to have so many moving parts come together after countless of hours of work spent over the preceeding months.

I am increbilty grateful to have had the opportunity to work with such motivated and inteligent individuals and proud to have taken such a large ownership over the design of this drone.

Space Enterprise @ Berkeley (SEB) Rocket Team

Propulsion Engineer

August 2021 - May 2022

SEB Website Link

For more than half of my time at UC Berkeley as an undergrad transfer student I was a member of the first collegiate rocket team to manufacture the a functioning liquid rocket engine. The team is focused around our mission to be the first team in the country to send a liquid-propelled rocket past the Karmen line,

or about 100km above Earth's surface. Our first attempt was dubbed Eureka-1.

Among other things, I spent most of my time working on the liquid oxygen plumbing/ feed sub-system assembly. Our engine relied on differential pressure from a high-pressure tank to 'push' propellant and oxydizer out of lower-pressure tanks: liquid-propane (LNg) and liquid-oxygen (LOx) respectively. Part of my responsability was to model, manufacture, and test the oxydizer feed assembly. During manufacturing industry

standard swagelok fittings, NPT fittings, compression fittings, and more were realied on, ensuring leaks were minimized leading to losses in pressure and performance.

Hotfire 2: Cart Test Stand

In order to gather valuable data and validate engine performance, the engine was assembled around a cart test stand, affectionately named LightBulb. In following safety procedures around highly energetic propellants, numerous hotfire tests were conducted at the Friends of Amatuer Rocketry Facility, or FAR, a controlled environment in the mojave Desert. We learned a lot in this first test, but still producing nearly 600 pounds of peak mean thrust.

This hotfire test marked the first liquid rocket engine to successfully produce thrust at the University of California, Berkeley, which was no small feat.

Hotfire 6: Vertical Test Stand

With a lot of lessons learned, a lot of modifications made, and some failed attempts (rapid unscheduled disassemblies as they say), 6 months later we were able to reliably fire the vertical test stand engine, producing nearly 1100 pounds of peak mean thrust.

How to avoid a hard-start... testing... COLD FLOWS!

Given the danger surrounding rocketry testing, that being maintianing a literal controlled explosion, a lot of testing had to be done with water, to test pintle injector atomization and early low-pressure leaks, and with an inert cryogenic fluid: liquid-nitrogen or LN2, to test for leaks and performance more closely resembling cryogenic, high-pressure conditions.

It was during these sometimes late night tests that we gathered valuable data related to droop pressure losses, thrust load cell accuracy, capacitive fill sensor accuracy, and much more performance related data. Also during these tests we were able to simulate and practice our safety and testing procedures effectively in preperation for hotfires.

The team is composed from a wide variety of backgrounds and disciplines, all passionate about space technology. These students are going on to be some of the greatest minds the world has ever seen and I am humbled to have learned from them and worked side-by-side with them for so long.
